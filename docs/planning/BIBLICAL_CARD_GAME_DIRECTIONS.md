# Biblical Card Game Directions

## Core Tension

There is a real design contradiction at the center of a biblical card game that borrows heavily from MTG:

> Are these sacred figures, or are they game pieces?

Traditional MTG combat assumes entities fight each other directly. Biblical narratives tend to care more about:

- faithfulness vs corruption
- covenant and judgment
- mercy and sacrifice
- exile, redemption, and fulfillment

That makes cards like Jesus, Mary, Moses, Abraham, and Isaac feel thematically awkward if they are just creatures attacking each other on a battlefield.

At the same time, the strongest parts of the concept are not direct combat themselves. The strongest parts are the expressive, high-impact biblical moments:

- "The Flood" wipes the board
- "Rapture" resets or extracts the faithful
- "Ultimate Resurrection" returns everyone from the grave
- Abraham enables multiplication, descendants, and mana growth
- Jesus can be removed early before returning as a stronger judging form

Those ideas are excellent because they translate theology and story into gameplay.

## What Seems Worth Preserving

The project already has a compelling design center:

- deckbuilding
- strong archetypal leaders
- explosive biblical event cards
- thematic engines tied to figures, stories, and eras
- memorable swing turns

This suggests the goal is not "make the Bible into MTG combat," but:

> Build a card game where biblical themes, stories, authorities, and judgments become game systems.

## Possible Directions

### 1. Lean Into Irreverence

Let biblical figures fight freely and embrace the absurdity.

Examples:

- Jesus fights Satan
- Abraham fights Isaac
- Moses fights Mary
- the whole world is stylized with anime, exaggerated, or symbolic presentation

Pros:

- fastest to prototype
- easiest to map onto existing MTG structure
- maximum mechanical freedom
- preserves splashy, funny, and absurd matchups

Cons:

- invites parody reading
- lowers theological weight
- may alienate players who want the game to feel more coherent or respectful

Recommendation:

- best used as a prototype or alternate mode, not the safest long-term identity
- if chosen, commit fully to the tone instead of half-presenting it as reverent

### 2. Reframe the Battle

Keep two-sided competition, but change what "battle" means.

The weaker version of this idea is "two theologians dueling," because it still leaves the question:

> Why is a theologian summoning a 3/3 Adam to clash with a 7/8 Gabriel?

A stronger version is to say players represent something more abstract:

- nations
- covenants
- churches
- spiritual authorities
- competing wills or alignments

Then cards represent:

- influence
- covenant
- judgment
- witness
- corruption
- fulfillment

This is more coherent than wizard-summoner framing, but it still needs a clean explanation of what cards are doing on the board.

Recommendation:

- promising as a reframe, especially if paired with a non-lethal combat substitute like influence or trials

### 3. Explore Alternate Game Styles

Move away from literal MTG combat while preserving deckbuilding and swingy card play.

Possible inspirations:

- scenario-driven deckbuilder
- tableau engine builder
- map/control game
- cooperative crisis game
- semi-cooperative survival game

Pros:

- creates more room for thematic consistency
- avoids sacred-character brawl awkwardness
- can still support massive biblical event cards

Cons:

- risks losing the immediacy and drama of board-based card combat if over-abstracted
- requires more invention than a direct MTG-style prototype

Recommendation:

- worthwhile if the system still supports "I cast The Flood" moments
- avoid drifting into a dry euro structure that removes the dramatic card identity

### 4. Lean Heavily Into Biblical Chronology

Structure the game around eras of divine history.

Examples:

- Creation
- Patriarchs
- Exodus
- Kingdom
- Christ
- Church
- Revelation

This could drive:

- campaign/scenario progression
- deck restrictions by era
- card transformations as promises are fulfilled
- new mechanics entering and old mechanics becoming obsolete

Pros:

- strong theological coherence
- excellent campaign structure
- naturally supports expansion content

Cons:

- may be too restrictive as the sole core structure
- chronology alone does not solve the combat problem

Recommendation:

- strongest as scenario or campaign scaffolding rather than the only game identity

### 5. Cooperative Game Against Boss Decks

Players cooperate against a much stronger enemy deck or set of enemy decks.

This may be the cleanest thematic solution.

Players could represent:

- leaders of faith
- tribes or covenant communities
- apostolic missions
- virtues
- redemptive factions

The enemy could represent:

- Satan
- Sin
- Death
- Babylon
- the Beast
- demonic hosts
- the Seven Deadly Sins

Example boss decks:

- Pride: self-buffing, punishes humility, forces comparison and domination
- Greed: treasure pressure, resource denial, corruption through gain
- Envy: copies or steals player strengths
- Wrath: repeated destruction and escalating punishment
- Death: attrition, graveyard denial, inevitability
- Deception: false copies, hidden threats, betrayal effects

Pros:

- removes the "Jesus fights Mary" problem
- aligns naturally with biblical themes
- preserves event-card spectacle
- allows strong leader identities without requiring sacrilegious pairings

Cons:

- less natural for direct PvP replay than standard MTG
- replayability must come from many enemy decks, scenarios, and modular encounter systems

Recommendation:

- one of the strongest directions
- likely the best fit for preserving biblical tone and mechanical excitement together

### 6. Use the Demon/Enemy Idea as a Reframe

This extends the cooperative idea and also improves competitive framing.

Instead of players being combat wizards, players could represent:

- souls
- churches
- communities
- covenant lines
- spiritual paths

Cards then become:

- figures
- events
- trials
- influences
- powers
- places
- prophecies

Conflict becomes:

- temptation
- resistance
- witness
- redemption
- corruption
- judgment

Recommendation:

- excellent as a thematic frame whether the game is cooperative, semi-cooperative, or asymmetrical

### 7. Replace "Attacking" With Something Else

This is probably the single most important mechanical reframe if the game stays board-centric.

Possible replacements for combat:

#### Influence

Units contest people, places, or nations for allegiance and control.

#### Faith vs Corruption

A tug-of-war over hearts, territories, or the state of the world.

#### Trials

Characters are assigned to trials like famine, persecution, temptation, exile, or idolatry rather than attacking each other directly.

#### Dominion

The struggle is over authority, witness, and spiritual ground instead of bodily harm.

#### Judgment Track

Actions accelerate or delay an approaching final resolution.

#### Covenant Engine

Long-term commitments, promises, and fulfillments matter more than damage racing.

Recommendation:

- best candidates are Influence, Trials, and Faith vs Corruption
- even a simple verb change from "attack" to "contest," "resist," or "witness" helps a lot

## Recommended Hybrid Direction

The strongest combined recommendation is:

### Core Identity

A deckbuilding card game with MTG-adjacent structure, but not literal sacred-character deathmatch combat.

### Narrative Frame

Players participate in the unfolding struggle between covenant and corruption, resisting demonic, sinful, imperial, or apocalyptic forces.

### Mechanical Priorities

- keep strong leaders
- keep high-impact event cards
- keep combo/archetype expression
- reduce or remove direct sacred-character brawling

### Opposition Model

Use one of these:

- cooperative against a shared Adversary deck
- semi-cooperative survival where everyone can lose, but one player is remembered most faithfully
- asymmetrical game with one Enemy player controlling corruption, demons, or empire

### Combat Replacement

Use:

- Influence
- Trials
- Dominion
- Faith vs Corruption

instead of simple life-total combat.

## A Strong Prototype Structure

If prototyping toward the most promising version, start here:

- 1-4 players
- each player chooses a leader
- each leader defines colors, factions, or gameplay identity
- a central Adversary deck reveals escalating threats each round
- players lose if Corruption, Despair, Tribulation, or Doom crosses a threshold
- players win by surviving to prophecy fulfillment, defeating a final boss, or completing covenant objectives

Possible card types:

- Figure
- Event
- Prophecy
- Covenant
- Trial
- Sin
- Power
- Site

This structure keeps the desired moments intact:

- "Cast The Flood, wipe nearly everything"
- "Ultimate Resurrection, return the faithful"
- "Jesus the Judge is about to return stronger"
- "Abraham goes wide through descendants and multiplication"

## Example Leader Identities

These identities feel thematic because they express what a figure does in the story rather than asking who they can physically beat in combat.

### Abraham

- ramp
- multiplication
- descendant tokens
- covenant scaling

### Moses

- control
- liberation
- law
- plagues
- protection for weaker allies

### David

- tempo
- underdog combat tricks
- worship/psalm synergy
- kingly momentum

### Solomon

- wisdom
- card draw
- treasure/resources
- temptation and idolatry risk

### Jesus, Teacher

- healing
- mercy
- support
- low-aggression but strong board influence

### Jesus, Judge

- transformed late-game form
- cleansing
- inevitability
- judgment payoff

### Paul

- conversion
- recursion
- turning former enemies into allies
- deck manipulation

## Why the Cooperative / Adversary Direction Stands Out

This direction solves multiple problems at once:

- sacred figures do not need to punch each other
- demonic, sinful, and apocalyptic opposition gives the game a natural enemy
- event cards like The Flood, Rapture, Exodus, Resurrection, and Final Judgment make immediate sense
- leaders can still support wildly different playstyles
- the game can feel biblical without becoming a pure teaching exercise

It also opens up clean expansion lanes:

- Deadly Sins enemy decks
- era-based scenario packs
- campaign play through biblical history
- covenant-focused player factions
- end-times and Revelation modules

## Recommended Path Forward

If choosing a practical direction for actual design work, the current recommendation is:

1. Make the base game cooperative or semi-cooperative against Adversary decks.
2. Reframe combat as Influence, Trials, Dominion, or Faith vs Corruption.
3. Keep biblical heroes as build-around leaders with strong thematic mechanics.
4. Use biblical chronology as campaign/scenario structure, not the only core mechanic.
5. Treat full irreverent sacred-figure battle mode as an optional prototype or alternate format rather than the main identity.

## Design Principle to Carry Forward

The game does not need to be a Bible reskin of MTG.

It can be something stronger:

> a card game where biblical theology, story, judgment, mercy, covenant, temptation, and fulfillment become gameplay systems.

## Useful Next Steps

The most productive follow-up design tasks would be:

1. define a resource system to replace or reinterpret mana
2. design a rules skeleton for non-lethal "combat"
3. create 5 example leaders and their archetypes
4. build 1 Adversary deck, such as Pride or Satan
5. prototype 10-20 cards around one leader plus one enemy deck
6. outline a turn structure for co-op or semi-co-op play
