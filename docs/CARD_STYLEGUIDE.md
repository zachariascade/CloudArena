# Cloud Arcanum Card Style Guide

This document captures editorial rules for naming and presenting cards so the set feels cohesive as it grows.

## 1. Goal

Card names should feel flavorful, memorable, and internally consistent with the universe, set, and source material they draw from.

The style guide is especially important for legendary figures, where a strong title can add fantasy texture without obscuring the character's identity.

## 2. General Naming Principles

- prefer names that are easy to scan and easy to remember
- make the most important identity readable at a glance
- let the card name carry flavor without requiring flavor text to do all the work
- avoid names that feel generic, interchangeable, or detached from the card's role
- keep names aligned with the card's mechanics, colors, and thematic job in the set

## 3. Legendary Figure Naming

When a card represents a known person, faction leader, champion, monster, spirit, deity, historical figure, or other named character:

- keep the figure's actual name in the card name
- do not replace a well-known figure with a purely descriptive title
- if the card needs more texture, add an epithet or title after the name

Preferred pattern:

`Name, Title`

Examples:

- `Michael, Seraph of the First Charge`
- `Gabriel, Trumpet of the Final Dawn`
- `Sarah, Laughter of Promise`
- `Abraham, Father of Nations`
- `Athena, Voice of the Citadel`
- `Kara, Ash-Wreathed Marshal`

Avoid:

- `Seraph of the First Charge` for Michael
- `Ash-Wreathed Marshal` when the actual character is Kara

## 4. Titles And Epithets

Titles should add specificity, not hide the subject.

Good titles usually do at least one of these things:

- reflect the character's role in the story or setting
- reinforce the card's color identity or mechanics
- create a memorable fantasy image
- distinguish one legendary character from another

Good title qualities:

- vivid
- role-driven
- concise
- respectful of the source material

Avoid titles that are:

- too generic, such as `Angel of Light`
- too modern in tone
- disconnected from the card's flavor or function
- misleading about the character's established identity

## 5. Source Tradition And Adaptation

When adapting material from religion, mythology, folklore, history, literature, or original setting lore:

- preserve the canonical or broadly recognized name when one exists
- let fantasy embellishment happen in the title, flavor text, mechanics, and art direction
- prefer role-consistent titles over arbitrary cool-sounding phrases
- be especially careful when drawing from traditions, franchises, or settings that carry strong audience expectations

This project does not need to be strictly academic, but it should feel intentional rather than careless.

## 6. Set Cohesion Guidance

Each set should develop a naming voice that feels shared across its cards.

Questions to ask:

- what tone defines this set: noble, tragic, cosmic, grim, mythic, gothic, strange, pulp, heroic
- what vocabulary belongs here and what vocabulary does not
- should names feel formal, poetic, brutal, mystical, militaristic, ancient, or plainspoken
- do cards of the same faction, species, court, order, or region sound like they belong together

Examples:

- a celestial set may favor radiant, heraldic, and exalted titles
- a gothic horror set may favor mournful, haunted, and aristocratic titles
- a sword-and-sorcery set may favor harsher, bolder, more immediate titles

For named characters in any set:

- keep the actual name first when the identity matters
- use the subtitle to express role, office, reputation, or mythic tone

## 7. Slug Rules

Card slugs should be derived from the final approved card name.

- use lowercase
- replace spaces and punctuation with underscores
- keep the slug readable
- include both the proper name and the title when the card uses `Name, Title`

Examples:

- `Michael, Seraph of the First Charge` -> `michael_seraph_of_the_first_charge`
- `Abraham, Father of Nations` -> `abraham_father_of_nations`

## 8. Review Checklist

Before accepting a card name:

- does it preserve the figure's identity when identity is important
- does it sound like it belongs in this set
- does it match the card's color pair or mechanical role
- does it avoid sounding generic
- does the slug match the final approved name

If the answer to any of these is no, revise the name before treating it as settled.
