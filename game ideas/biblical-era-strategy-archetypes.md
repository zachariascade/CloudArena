# Biblical Era Strategy Archetypes

## Core Idea

Map gameplay archetypes onto biblical eras, characters, and groups so each part of scripture has a clear mechanical identity.

The key design move is:
- start with a playstyle
- assign it to the biblical era that best expresses it
- let that era's cards mostly reinforce one another

This keeps theme and mechanics aligned instead of treating biblical flavor as a skin over generic cards.

## Design Rules

### Pool-Bound Synergy

Most cards should primarily synergize with their own era, tribe, or mechanic family.

Examples:
- Patriarch cards reward descendants, households, tribes, and multiplication
- Gospel cards reward miracles, healing, faith, and resurrection
- Kings cards reward leadership, equipment, combat, and fortification
- Revelation cards reward judgment, seals, escalation, and huge summons

Cross-pool synergy can exist, but it should be limited and intentional.

### Why This Helps

- reduces wild interaction complexity
- nudges players toward readable archetypes
- makes balancing easier
- gives each pool a stronger identity
- keeps discovery inside understandable lanes

### Recommended Structure Per Pool

Each biblical pool should have:
- a primary identity label
- a signature mechanic family
- a few internal enablers
- a few internal payoffs
- only a small number of crossover bridges

## Archetype Menu

These do not need to come only from *Magic*. They can also borrow from roguelike deckbuilders, RPG parties, tactical games, and other strategy genres.

- `Go wide`: many small units, swarm pressure, shared buffs
- `Go tall / Voltron`: one champion or a few elite units stacked with buffs or equipment
- `Midrange value`: efficient, durable units that win through steady quality
- `Control`: stall, deny, and win through inevitability
- `Ramp`: build resources early to unlock oversized plays
- `Big summons`: giant entities and dramatic late-game arrivals
- `Graveyard recursion`: restore or replay what was lost
- `Self-sacrifice`: give up life, units, or resources for stronger payoffs
- `Healing / sustain`: repair damage, cleanse, and outlast
- `Faith / risk scaling`: commit before payoff and get rewarded for trust
- `Combo / engine`: assemble linked pieces that generate repeated value
- `Tempo`: seize initiative and stay ahead with short-term edges
- `Fortification`: defend with walls, guards, shields, and refuge
- `Status / affliction`: weaken enemies through plague, fear, corruption, or judgment
- `Stance switching`: shift between modes like worship, prophecy, zeal, or defense
- `Hand cycling / insight`: draw, filter, scry, and sculpt turns
- `Relic / artifact synergy`: build around persistent objects, sacred items, or spaces
- `Party / formation synergy`: reward a team made of complementary roles
- `Burst turn / exhaust`: save up for one explosive turn
- `Life as resource`: spend health or suffering for power
- `Chaos / rebellion`: instability, unpredictability, betrayals, and fragmentation

## Era Mapping

### Early World Genesis

Primary identity:
- creation into corruption
- remnant after judgment

Secondary mechanics:
- growth into collapse
- board reset and rebuild

Key figures:
- Adam and Eve
- Cain and Abel
- Noah
- Babel humanity

Notes:
- This era should feel primal, unstable, and foundational.
- It differs from Patriarchs by focusing on collapse, cleansing, and survival rather than healthy multiplication.

### Patriarchs

Primary identity:
- go wide

Secondary mechanics:
- faith / risk scaling
- tribe-matters synergy

Key figures:

- Jacob

- the tribes of Israel

## Quick Examples

- Early World Genesis: corruption, cleansing reset, remnant rebuild
- Patriarchs: descendants, tribe synergy, multiplication
- Exodus: provision, survival, plagues
- Judges: tempo, daring plays, instability
- Kings: champions, equipment, combat leadership
- Prophets: control, foresight, judgment
- Temple: relics, sacrifice, ritual engines
- Gospels: miracles, healing, resurrection
- Acts: party synergy, mission pressure, resilience
- Revelation: huge summons, final judgments, inevitability

## Open Questions

- Should each era have one primary archetype or one primary plus one secondary?
- How much crossover is healthy before identities blur?
- Which mechanics should belong to only one era?
- Are these pools factions, deck themes, draft lanes, or run packages?

## Summary

The strongest direction is to give each biblical era its own tightly bounded pool, archetype, and mechanical fantasy. That keeps the game readable, supports flavorful deck identities, and avoids overly tangled card interactions.
